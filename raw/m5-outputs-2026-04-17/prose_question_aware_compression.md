Context compression is a critical optimization for Large Language Models (LLMs) operating within long-context windows or Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines. While traditional compression methods focus on reducing token counts based on generic perplexity or entropy, question-aware context compression introduces a paradigm shift by leveraging the user query to guide the selection of informative tokens. This ensures that the compressed prompt retains the semantic signal necessary for task-specific reasoning.

LongLLMLingua represents a sophisticated approach through question-conditioning. Unlike vanilla compression, which treats all context as equally important, LongLLMLingua utilizes the query to compute the conditional perplexity of tokens. By evaluating how much each token contributes to the likelihood of the query, the algorithm can prune tokens that are irrelevant to the specific task. This method effectively minimizes the loss of critical information by prioritizing tokens that have high mutual information with the query, thereby maintaining the "answerability" of the context even at high compression ratios.

A related strategy is the query-aware retrieval-then-compression pipeline. In this architecture, the query acts as the primary filter during the retrieval stage, followed by a secondary compression stage that is also query-sensitive. The compression step does not merely reduce the volume of retrieved chunks; it selectively retains segments that exhibit high semantic overlap or relevance to the query embedding. This two-stage process optimizes the context window by discarding noise introduced by the retrieval of semi-relevant documents, ensuring that the remaining tokens are highly concentrated with task-relevant data.

More lightweight approaches rely on statistical or structural heuristics. IDF-style relevance scoring treats compression as a feature selection problem. By calculating the importance of tokens based on their frequency in the context versus their frequency in a broader corpus (or the query), the system can prune common, non-informative tokens and focus on rare, high-information tokens. Complementing this, keep-near-entity heuristics prioritize the preservation of tokens surrounding identified named entities or key terms found in the query. This method assumes that the answer to a query is often anchored to specific entities, and by protecting the local context of these entities, the model maintains the structural integrity of the facts.

Comparing these methods reveals a fundamental trade-off between computational overhead and semantic precision. LongLLMLingua and query-aware compression offer superior accuracy and reasoning capabilities because they capture deep semantic dependencies, but they incur significant latency due to the need for additional model passes, often utilizing smaller, faster LLMs to compute perplexity. In contrast, IDF-style scoring and entity-based heuristics are computationally inexpensive and highly scalable, making them suitable for real-time, high-throughput applications. However, these statistical methods are "blind" to complex linguistic nuances and may inadvertently prune subtle but critical contextual cues. The selection of a compression strategy must therefore balance the cost of inference against the required precision of the retrieved response.
